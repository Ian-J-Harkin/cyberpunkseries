FIRST IMPRESSIONS

First Impressions: What You've Got Working For You
The Agent J / Jim Rockford fusion is genuinely fresh. Rockford gave us the detective who loses as often as he wins, who gets punched, scammed, and underpaid, but keeps his dignity and humour intact. J gives you the physical confidence, the wit, the refusal to be intimidated by things that should intimidate him. Together you get someone who'll mouth off to a lunar corporate enforcer and then have to run very fast. That's a show. That's a character.
The moon-as-oil-rig economy is also the most grounded, believable worldbuilding choice you could make, and ground-level believability is what separates good lighter sci-fi from forgettable lighter sci-fi. It gives you class as subtext without ever having to lecture about it.

The World as I'm Reading It
Near-future Earth, say 2070-2090 range. Not apocalyptic, not utopian. More like... tired. The infrastructure works, mostly. There's a grid — biometric, financial, social credit adjacent — and most people are on it because the alternative is genuinely hard. But the alternative exists, and whole communities have built lives in the gaps and cracks.
The moon is real, staffed, industrially productive, and owned by a consortium that isn't quite a government. Workers go up on 18-month contracts, come back with money that makes them briefly rich in their old neighbourhood and permanently alien to it. Some go back. Some stay. Some come back changed in ways they don't talk about.
Your protagonist — let's call him Dex for now, placeholder — operates in the friction zone between all of these worlds. Licensed enough to get through doors. Unlicensed enough to have useful friends. Too broke to be picky, too principled (in his own idiosyncratic way) to take every job.

The Protagonist: Nailing the Voice
Before plot, the voice has to be locked. Because you're right that the tone of narration should reflect him entirely. Here's how I'd pitch that voice in prose terms:
Think Elmore Leonard writing Will Smith. Short sentences when things get fast. Long digressive ones when he's waiting, bored, observing. Funny but never trying to be funny — the humour comes from his genuine, slightly baffled take on a world that has gotten objectively ridiculous. He notices things a brooding hero wouldn't notice. The brand of synth-coffee someone's drinking. The way a corporate lawyer's shoes are too new. He's not cynical — cynicism is exhausted, and Dex is never exhausted, he's just perpetually unimpressed while somehow remaining curious.
The 7w8 energy means his narration should have this quality of forward momentum even when he's standing still. He's always already thinking about the next move, the exit, the angle. That's the interiority. Externally he's the guy who makes it all look easy, which is the joke, because it is never easy.

Series Architecture: What I'd Propose
Format: Standalone episodes with a serialised undercurrent. Each book is a case. The case resolves. But the world gets slightly more complicated with each one, and Dex's position in it shifts.
The Central Tension Arc across the series: Dex starts as genuinely off the grid by choice, scrambling but free. By series end — if you want a long arc — he's been pulled onto the grid against his will, given official status, resources, obligations, and finds that success is its own kind of trap. The series is about what it costs to remain yourself when the world keeps trying to categorise you.

Option B: "Off the Books"
Someone has been living entirely off the grid for eleven years in a major city. No biometric trail, no financial trace, no registered address. Dex is hired — cheaply — by what appears to be a family welfare case, worried elderly parents. Routine work.
The twist: the person doesn't want to be found, has built something remarkable in the cracks of the city, and finding them will destroy it. Dex has to decide whether "I was hired to do a job" is sufficient moral justification.
Thematic engine: Off-grid life as genuine alternative society. What does freedom cost, and who pays for it? The Dex character's own relationship with the grid is complicated here — he's partly off it, and this case holds a mirror up to choices he hasn't fully examined.
Tone calibration: Lighter, more comedic, more intimate. The "case" is almost a MacGuffin. Really it's about community, chosen family, urban survival. The off-grid community should feel genuinely appealing — not grimy survivalism but something closer to a jazz musician's version of urban life.



Recurring World Elements I'd Establish Early
A few things I'd want seeded in book one regardless of which direction you go:
Dex's workspace — not an office, something more provisional. A converted unit, a floating arrangement, something that reflects his relationship with permanence.
A lunar returnee contact — someone who went up, came back, never quite reintegrated. Dex's window into that world before he ever has to go there himself.
The grid checkpoint as recurring motif — every time Dex passes through one, we feel the friction between the world he moves through and the world that's trying to track him. Small moments, but they accumulate.
One genuinely funny recurring antagonist — not a villain, more of an obstacle with a personality. A grid compliance officer, a corporate fixer who keeps turning up, a rival skip-tracer who is everything Dex isn't.

Where is your instinct pulling you? I have strong feelings about Option B having the richest character material for a first novel, but Option A gives you the most series architecture to build on. Based on the collection of documents from the Future Fiction Academy, I have applied architectural principles to extract a structured hierarchy and tool flow for AI-assisted writing.
1. The AI Writing Hierarchy
The concepts in these documents naturally form a three-tier hierarchy that moves from high-level vision to specific technical implementation.
Tier 1: Strategic Governance (The "Compass" Layer)
North Star Statement: A high-level vision that keeps the project on track and prevents the AI from deviating into generic tropes.
World Compass: A set of governing rules for the story's universe to maintain consistency and prevent "spoilers" where the AI reveals things it shouldn't know yet.
Tier 2: Structural Logic (The "Blueprint" Layer)
Goal vs. Theme: Determining whether a multi-plotline story is unified by an external shared goal or an internal thematic thread.
Constraint Systems: Establishing the "physics" and "logic" of the world so the AI simulates the story rather than just generating text.
Self-Editing Outlines: A recursive workflow where the AI critiques and refines its own structural plans before any prose is written.
Tier 3: Tactical Execution (The "Engine" Layer)
Prompt Sequences: Pre-planned "roadmaps" that carry context from one stage to the next without manual rebuilding.
Character Archetyping: Using AI to iterate on psychological depth and "arc versions" (how a character acts in different acts of the story).

2. The Master Tool Flow (Linear Chain)
By chaining these unrelated concepts together, we can construct a robust end-to-end "Tool Flow" for producing an AI-assisted manuscript:
Vision Phase: Craft the North Star and World Compass to define the boundaries of the collaboration.
Architecture Phase: Decide between Goal-driven or Theme-driven plot structures for multi-thread narratives.
Simulation Phase: Input Constraint System Prompts to teach the AI the specific rules of the magic, technology, or social systems.
Iterative Planning Phase: Run a Self-Editing Outline loop where the AI acts as a developmental editor on its own generated plot.
Character Deployment: Use AI to generate Arc versions and specific dialogue styles for the main cast.
Production Phase: Execute Prompt Sequences to move from a validated outline to a first draft chapter-by-chapter, maintaining context throughout.

3. Collaboration Models (Stack Styles)
The documents suggest different ways to "stack" the human and AI roles depending on the desired outcome:
The AI Partner Model: The author and AI collaborate as equals, with AI often acting as a "third partner" to help two human authors blend their voices or keep lore straight.
The CEO Model: The author serves as the "CEO" of their business, using AI as a highly efficient staff for planning and organization while retaining all final creative judgment.
The Thinking Model Stack: Utilizing "thinking" models (like o1 or DeepSeek) specifically for the Constraint System phase to handle complex logical simulations.

4. Specialized Workflows
Short Story Collection Flow: Focuses on thematic cohesion across different protagonists and tonal registers to avoid "tonal whiplash" in a published collection.
The "No-Rebuild" Flow: Emphasizes using sequences to carry context forward, eliminating the need to re-explain the story to the AI every time a new session starts.
